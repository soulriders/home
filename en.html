Development Narrative

Flying Car: From Dream to Reality

Everyone dreams of a flying car at least once. So did we. We believed the tiltrotor could be the aircraft that makes that dream real. But in 1992, the crash of the Osprey (V-22) raised one question for us: to fly safely over cities where people live, wouldn't it need to be a far safer tiltrotor?

An Old Dream, Three Barriers

For that dream to become urban transportation, three problems had to be solved at once: take off and land in place, fly far, and move safely between those two modes. No aircraft has ever combined all three.

The Possibility of the Lambda Engine

It began with calculation. In 2006, looking at Korea’s independently developed Lambda automotive engine, we asked: could an aircraft fly with this engine? Counting seats from engine weight and output gave an answer: a Gamma engine for two seats, a Lambda engine for three. On paper, an aircraft built from automotive parts was already achievable.

The Invention of the Asynchronous Tiltrotor

The remaining problem was safety. We drew and erased more than 200 hover takeoff and landing aircraft forms. At the end, we arrived at a structure that tilts each rotor separately — the asynchronous tiltrotor — and filed the patent in 2020.

After that, the company’s time went into humanoid robot development. The aircraft was folded away in a drawer. One day, while preparing robot mass production, the patent registration notice arrived for that idea in the drawer. A forgotten idea had returned as a right.

2026, Completion of the SDA Architecture

The registration notice wasn't a cause for celebration — it was a question. Can this structure truly open the sky? Starting from the first 2020 patent, we redesigned a patent network that connects every phase of operation from takeoff to emergency landing. The system completed in 2026 is the SDA architecture — five core technologies and about 25 inventions.

An Aircraft That Competes with Truck Freight Rates

SDA will not remain an idea on drawings. The flying car we dreamed of as children is now preparing to take off as an aircraft that competes with truck freight rates. It begins with cargo. At the end of that path is the day it carries people.

Certification Schedule

eVTOL certification has dragged on for years — we addressed the certification requirements at the design stage.

Certification is what matters most here. Transparent, predictable certification is not out of reach.

The reason the valuations of eVTOL companies like Joby fluctuate is simple: no one can lock down the certification schedule. The market reads this not as a technology risk, but as uncertainty over how much more capital will be burned.

eVTOL must pass the following two FAA regulatory requirements.

  • Deterministic control: DO-178C
  • Power-off emergency landing: PL.2105(g) under FAA AC 21.17-4

We designed the aircraft to satisfy both requirements from the design stage.

1. It responds only in predefined ways (DO-178C)

Conventional aircraft respond to each gust through real-time computation, so the result cannot be predicted 100% in advance. Our aircraft precomputes the answer for each condition and only switches immediately, making the result provable to certification authorities in advance.

2. It lands under control even when the engine is off (PL.2105(g))

Even when the engine is off, control does not stop. In the extreme case of losing all power, the eight rotors create differences in air resistance — differential drag — forming virtual control surfaces. We designed a fail-safe that controls direction and trajectory to the end and performs a glide landing without power. SDA is an aircraft designed directly for PL.2105(g).

3. Certification-target components are reduced to about 1/100

Conventional aircraft have hundreds of failure points because of control surfaces and actuators, and each must be proven. We removed mechanical control surfaces themselves, dramatically reducing the components subject to certification.

4. We build trust with cargo before people

Instead of spending capital first on crewed-aircraft certification, we accumulate large volumes of real flight data through cargo transport. An accident-free operating record carries more weight than paperwork and accelerates later crewed certification.

In conclusion, we simplified computation, reduced parts, and resolved emergency response through design. Certification is no longer unpredictable R&D, but a procedure executed in a defined sequence.

IP LOGIC Why This Structure: Five Design Logics
01

Why Tiltrotor: Expanding Market Size

To enter the urban air market, hover takeoff and landing capability is essential; to expand into wider mobility markets, wings that can fly far are essential.

Operational efficiency is maximized when there are no dead rotors creating only drag in flight. This is the fundamental reason existing tiltrotor-based eVTOL companies carry both high valuations and uncertainty.

02

Why Independent Tiltrotor: Predictable Certification Timeline

Conventional tiltrotors rely on complex probabilistic control, making passage through stringent software certification standards such as DO-178C uncertain. This is the fundamental reason existing tiltrotor-based eVTOL companies carry both high valuations and uncertainty.

Independent control makes every flight response traceable and reproducible. Because the flight algorithm is deterministic, certification time and budget can be predicted.

03

Why Virtual Control Surfaces: Lower Manufacturing and Maintenance Cost

Physical control surfaces and mechanical actuators are removed, and flight is controlled through software-based rotor control.

With mechanical linkages removed, fully automated production of wings and fuselage becomes possible, bringing aircraft price down toward 10% of conventional levels.

As moving parts fall to one-hundredth, wear and failure factors decrease, reducing operating maintenance cost and maintenance time.

04

Power-Off Flight Capability: Entry Condition for the Regulatory System

FAA guidance (AC 21.17-4 Appendix A, PL.2105(g)) states that controlled emergency landing capability through glide or autorotation is required even when power is lost. SDA builds the power-off control logic for an aircraft with no moving wing parts through rotor-by-rotor autorotation and differential drag.

Actual operating approval can only be obtained once this legal standard is met.

05

SDA Integrated Value: Price Competition with Ground Freight

SDA is an architecture that simultaneously achieves deterministic flight control, power-off emergency landing capability based on autorotation and differential drag, and extreme parts simplification.

It secures high safety while lowering production and maintenance costs — competing directly with ground freight markets on price, beyond the aviation sector itself.

Strategic Essay

Lambda's Essential Competitive Advantage

We removed complex machinery and placed the flight capabilities an aircraft needs into one structure.

A software-defined aircraft that removes engineering complexity while preserving aircraft utility

Lambda's advantage does not lie in vertical takeoff and landing alone, nor in fixed-wing cruise alone. Tiltrotors, distributed electric propulsion, and tandem wings already exist individually.

Lambda's essential advantage is the structural removal of what makes conventional aircraft difficult.

Conventional aircraft add mechanisms, control surfaces, links, hydraulics, variable-pitch devices, and complex controls to gain performance and functions. More functions mean more parts, harder manufacturing and maintenance, and more failure modes and certification items.

Lambda was designed in the opposite direction.

Instead of adding more machinery, it delivers the required flight functions through independent tilting of identical fixed-pitch propulsors and software flight modes.

This sharply lowers engineering difficulty while retaining VTOL, fixed-wing cruise, transition, attitude control, high-lift flight, glide, and emergency-flight capability in one simple structure.

1. It did not solve the hard problem; it removed the problem itself

Lambda does not improve conventional aircraft complexity; it avoids the structures that create it.

Without traditional control surfaces such as ailerons, elevators, and rudders, the following disappear together:

  • Control-surface aerodynamic design
  • Hinge-moment calculations
  • Control-surface flutter verification
  • Control-surface servos and actuators
  • Links and cables
  • Backlash and clearance management
  • Control-surface internal wiring
  • Control-surface jamming and breakage modes
  • Control-surface manufacturing and assembly
  • Repeated analysis and retesting after control-surface changes

There is no helicopter-style swashplate or variable-pitch mechanism.

Each propulsor uses a fixed-pitch propeller and BLDC motor; the complete propeller, motor, and nacelle module rotates around a simple hinge.

Complex rotor hubs, pitch links, swashplates, hydraulics, and precision mechanical controls are unnecessary.

Lambda did not improve the most expensive and difficult parts of an aircraft. It removed them.

2. It controls flight with propulsor direction and thrust instead of control surfaces

Lambda's eight propulsors are not merely power sources. Each is an independent flight-control device that changes thrust magnitude and direction.

Roll, pitch, yaw, climb, descent, acceleration, and deceleration are produced directly by combining propulsor output and tilt angle, not indirectly through control surfaces.

This structure provides the following without separate control surfaces:

  • Vertical takeoff and landing
  • Hover
  • Low-speed attitude control
  • Cruise attitude control
  • Transition flight
  • High-lift flight
  • Virtual flaps
  • Attitude control using differential drag
  • Windmilling and autorotative flight
  • Attitude control during power-off glide

Conventional aircraft require separate surfaces and mechanisms for each function. Lambda changes the angle and power allocation of the same propulsors.

Adding flight functions does not require continuously adding hardware.

3. Asynchronous tilting structurally lowers transition-flight difficulty

The hardest phase of a conventional tiltrotor is the transition from vertical flight to cruise.

When all propulsors tilt together, lift sources and control methods change at once. Rotor lift, wing lift, attitude, thrust direction, and stall margin must all be managed simultaneously.

Lambda does not transition every propulsor at once.

Some propulsors retain vertical lift and attitude control while others tilt first to create forward thrust. Once speed and wing lift are established, the remaining propulsors transition sequentially.

A propulsor group always remains to support and control the aircraft during transition.

Lambda turns one complex continuous transition into stepwise movement between stable states.

  • Vertical-flight state
  • Transition of selected propulsors
  • Forward-thrust formation
  • Airspeed increase
  • Wing-lift formation
  • Transition of remaining propulsors
  • Entry into cruise

At each step, the propulsors responsible for lift, forward thrust, and attitude control are explicit.

There is no need to optimize every variable simultaneously through an unstable transition region.

Asynchronous tilting does not suppress the tiltrotor transition problem with a stronger controller. It simplifies the transition structure itself.

4. MMA calls validated flight structures instead of optimizing in real time

Lambda does not calculate countless actuator combinations every moment to find an optimum.

It selects predefined, validated control matrices according to flight state, speed, attitude, propulsor status, and constraints.

Each MMA explicitly defines:

  • Which propulsors provide lift
  • Which propulsors provide forward thrust
  • Which propulsors control roll
  • Which propulsors control pitch
  • Which propulsors control yaw
  • Which propulsors tilt
  • The output range of each propulsor
  • Conditions for switching to the next MMA
  • The alternate MMA used after a propulsor failure

The control system calls a defined control structure according to aircraft state.

This uses less computation than complex real-time optimization, produces repeatable results, and makes test conditions and outcomes traceable.

The same state and input use the same MMA and control structure.

This is the core of Lambda's deterministic flight.

Certification authorities can be shown in advance each propulsor's role, mode-transition conditions, and alternate structures after failures.

This improves the predictability of development and certification schedules as well as control performance.

5. It repeats identical fore and aft wings and eight identical propulsion modules

Lambda's fore and aft wings are not separate dedicated designs. The same wing component is repeated front and rear.

Common wing geometry, span, chord, propulsor spacing, and mounting structure reduce:

  • Wing design cost
  • Mold cost
  • Tooling cost
  • Structural-test cost
  • Production-equipment cost
  • Parts inventory
  • Maintenance training
  • Replacement-part varieties
  • Quality-control items

The eight tilt nacelles use identical motors, propellers, hinges, mounts, and control interfaces.

Instead of multiple dedicated propulsion devices, one validated propulsion module is repeated eight times.

More propulsors don't add much mechanical complexity, because they are eight identical simple modules rather than eight different devices.

After a failure, modules can be replaced instead of disassembling complex components in the field.

Lambda production and maintenance resemble automotive parts or industrial modules more than conventional aircraft.

6. Fixed-pitch BLDC propulsion and a simple tilt structure lower propulsion-system difficulty

Lambda's propulsion system contains little specialized, complex aviation machinery.

Its core components are:

  • Fixed-pitch propeller
  • BLDC motor
  • ESC
  • Simple rotary hinge
  • Tilt actuator
  • Externally mounted support

Without variable pitch, pitch links, bearings, hubs, actuators, and related maintenance items disappear.

Thrust is adjusted through motor RPM and nacelle angle instead of precision mechanical control.

BLDC motors and ESCs are mature technologies used at scale in vehicles, industrial robots, drones, and electric machinery.

Lambda does not develop a new powerplant from scratch. It packages mature electric components into a simple propulsion module and repeats it.

7. It combines multirotor simplicity with fixed-wing cruise efficiency

Multirotors make VTOL and hover simple, but their rotors must support aircraft weight even in cruise.

Energy efficiency falls sharply as speed and range increase.

Lambda uses distributed electric propulsion for takeoff and landing, and fixed wings support its weight in cruise.

In cruise, propulsors provide forward thrust to overcome drag rather than continuously lifting the aircraft.

Flight control and propulsion remain as simple as a multirotor while cruise benefits from fixed-wing lift-to-drag ratio.

Lambda is neither an electrified helicopter nor a conventional airplane with VTOL motors added.

It uses multirotor physics in vertical flight and fixed-wing physics in cruise, clearly separating the roles of each state.

8. Tandem-wing stagger makes the structure part of flight control

Lambda places identical fore and aft wings at different heights in a tandem-wing structure.

The stagger between them is not merely visual.

It supports pitch stability, lift-center distribution, propulsor placement, and differential-drag control.

Aircraft geometry and propulsor control create the required stability and authority without adding horizontal and vertical tails, elevators, or rudders.

The structure itself becomes part of the flight-control system.

Required functions are obtained without adding tail structures, control surfaces, actuators, associated production, maintenance, or certification items.

9. Manufacturing and maintenance move beyond conventional aircraft production

Conventional aircraft are expensive to produce because of complex surfaces, dedicated parts, and manual assembly.

Lambda uses a simple fuselage, identical fore and aft wings, eight identical propulsion modules, external harnesses, and repeatable assembly.

This structure supports:

  • Automated composite-panel forming
  • Surface-mounted component assembly
  • Repeated installation of identical modules
  • External harness assembly
  • Automated inspection by process
  • Module-level replacement
  • Inventory centered on fewer parts

Aircraft are generally expensive because production volumes are low.

Lambda repeats common parts even at low volume and can approach automotive and industrial economies of scale as volume grows.

Maintenance is different as well.

After a failure, motors, ESCs, tilt units, sensors, and control modules are replaced rather than complex control systems being disassembled in the field.

This reduces maintenance and downtime and lowers dependence on highly specialized labor.

10. Lower aircraft complexity also lowers certification difficulty

Aircraft certification can cost far more than manufacturing the parts.

Every new control surface, mechanism, actuator, or control method adds failure modes, structural tests, software verification, flight tests, and maintenance procedures.

Lambda was designed from the outset to reduce certification scope.

  • No control surfaces
  • No variable-pitch mechanism
  • No hydraulic system
  • No complex mechanical control system
  • Repeated identical propulsion modules
  • Explicit control structure by flight mode
  • State-based discrete matrices
  • Deterministic control
  • Predefined alternate MMAs for failures

The configurations and operating logic to be certified become simpler.

Lambda certification is closer to validating limited flight modes and explicit transition conditions one by one than explaining an entire complex aircraft as one giant system.

Propulsor roles and control authority are predefined by flight state, and the same state uses the same control structure.

While competing systems face lengthy software and systems certification due to complex mechanisms and real-time black-box control, Lambda can plan a shorter, more predictable schedule through deterministic flight and fixed MMA transitions.

11. The remaining development work involves filling in test values, not discovering unknown physics

Lambda still requires testing and validation.

The nature of the remaining work differs from conventional eVTOL.

Key validation items include:

  • Actual rotor-wing interference
  • Lift sharing between fore and aft wings
  • Thrust by flight mode
  • Transition timing and speed
  • Center-of-gravity envelope
  • Attitude-control gains
  • Alternate MMA after propulsor failure
  • Actual lift-to-drag ratio
  • Actual range and power consumption
  • Maintenance intervals under recurring operation
  • Noise and vibration
  • Structural loads and fatigue life

These do not require discovering new physical laws or uncertain flight concepts.

They are engineering tasks: build and instrument the aircraft, obtain test values, and calibrate the software.

The flight methodology, propulsor roles by mode, and transition sequence are already defined.

Development centers on confirming values and tolerances for a defined structure, not searching for a new principle.

13. Lambda's greatest advantage is the complete industrial structure, not one performance metric

Lambda's advantage is not a single number for speed, range, or payload.

Its real advantage is that the following connect in one structure:

  • Low engineering difficulty.
  • Fewer parts.
  • Repeated common parts.
  • Easier manufacturing automation.
  • Simpler maintenance.
  • Deterministic control.
  • Explicit flight modes and transition conditions.
  • A bounded certification scope.
  • Lower aircraft cost.
  • High-utilization recurring operation.
  • Fixed-wing cruise efficiency.
  • Software-expandable functions.
  • Cargo operations and crewed expansion on one platform.

Conventional eVTOL accepts aircraft complexity to gain performance.

Lambda removes complexity while retaining required flight functions and aircraft utility.

It does not make a difficult aircraft cheaper after the fact. It selects a flight method, airframe, and production system that can be simple and affordable from the start.

Conclusion

Lambda is not more complex than a conventional tiltrotor.

It removes difficult problems from tiltrotors, helicopters, multirotors, and fixed wings, integrating the required functions into identical fixed-pitch propulsors and software flight modes.

It has no control surfaces, swashplate, variable pitch, or complex mechanical control system.

The fore and aft wings are identical, as are the eight propulsors.

Transition occurs sequentially while a propulsor group continues supporting and controlling the aircraft.

Control calls validated MMAs according to aircraft state instead of seeking a black-box optimum every moment.

Production resembles repeated module assembly and automation more than traditional hand-built aircraft manufacturing.

Maintenance resembles module replacement more than disassembly of complex mechanisms.

Certification becomes the validation of limited modes and deterministic transition conditions one by one, rather than explaining numerous mechanisms and uncertain control logic.

The product expands into two weight classes and three models.

The 599 kg uncrewed model carries 300 kg and targets an aircraft price of about KRW 40 million.

The 890 kg uncrewed model carries 475 kg and targets about KRW 100 million.

The four-seat crewed 890 kg variant targets about KRW 200 million and enters passenger markets with technology and flight data accumulated in cargo operation.

Lambda's essence is not technology that makes high-performance aircraft difficult.

It lowers engineering difficulty toward drones and industrial electric machinery while extending utility toward fixed-wing aircraft and regional transport infrastructure.

Lambda's strongest advantage can be stated in one sentence.

Lambda is a software-defined aircraft that transfers aircraft functions from complex machinery to independent propulsors and software flight modes, lowering the difficulty and cost of manufacturing, maintenance, certification, and operation together.

Once validated in recurring operation, Lambda goes beyond competing with conventional eVTOL.

It changes the cost structure of building aircraft and operating air transport itself.
